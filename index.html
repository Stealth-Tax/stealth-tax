Forgotten Axioms - Leave the Sophistry Behind

Time has the ultimate scarcity; we only have a finite amount. Resources are scarce to varying degrees, though despite many proclamations to the contrary, we have yet to run out of a single commodity. Time can be spent productively or wasted, but importantly, this is a subjective judgement. It depends on many factors, which vary from person to person, such as their preferences and time horizons. How is this all coordinated? Could a committee orchestrate the near-infinite iterations of resource and time usage in an economy?

Viewing prices as costs is instructive. Prices convey in a single number the information about scarcity required to coordinate supply and demand. If war breaks out in an oil producing nation, the oil price is likely to go up. Nobody has to know that war has broken out in order to get the information that the oil supply has been harmed.

A farmer can plant seeds and wait for the harvest, investing time and effort along the way to ensure the crops grow. The yield is the amount of crop the farmer ends up with, which he can sell. He will also have seeds for next season to continue the business. Alternatively, the farmer could avoid all the effort and sell the seeds immediately, to have money right away. But this strategy is unlikely to be conducive to carrying on in the business of farming. In this analogy, seeds are the farmer’s capital. His labour, land, tractor and tools are also capital.

‘Free’ public services, such as healthcare, are not free in any sense other than the fact that the recipient does not pay at the point of receiving the service. The staff, machines and buildings all have to be paid for somehow.

Fiat is money by decree, or government money. Its value proposition comes solely from the government's ability to exercise force. Its value relative to other currencies relies on the credibility of the treasury to manage a feasible budget, and the central bank to ‘control’ short term interest rates effectively. Fiat allows governments to print currency for their largesse, devaluing money already in existence. No wealth is created through this process. In fact, it is destroyed by inflation. The government and its favoured parties benefit through rent seeking.

To the extent that fiat is legal tender within the state, it is a claim on wealth within the state’s borders. By adding more money without producing any more wealth, the wealth in existence increases in nominal terms. This is one of the reasons why house prices have seen such extraordinary gains in nominal money terms despite the real value of a house not changing markedly - they are an ‘inflation hedge’.

Productivity is output per person. We can each produce more as a result of technology. Innovation drives technology. More innovation happens with less regulation and a competitive environment that enables risk-taking. Governments do not face the market test and are hampered by wasteful bureaucracy. They also incentivise rent seeking behaviour, such as lobbying for regulation to stifle competition, or extracting tribute from politically unpopular companies to darlings of the time (see carbon credits).

There are many who enjoy profitable positions in the current system. It is convenient for a government to be able to spend without recourse. Those who can lobby and influence policy and have access to central bankers, ‘win’ government contracts, and get rewarded with state mandated monopolies.

This is a common excuse for government recklessness. In reality, all reckless nations are taking part in capital destruction. Clearly, it is possible for all nations to destroy their capital stock simultaneously. Though politicians and their fans can spin it whichever way suits their re-election campaign, this is doing unspeakable damage to the quality of life we can expect in the future.

Rather than relying on fiat money that has to be devalued by design, you can store value in an asset that cannot be inflated. Bitcoin has a fixed supply, relies on no third parties, cannot have its monetary policy altered, and most importantly, it works. No government or special interest can stop it. Criticisms of price volatility are old paradigm. As more users join the network and more people wish to transact in Bitcoin, a new economy aside from fiat is emerging. The potential of this new economy is too great to ignore, and its superior money-network will become more valuable in real terms.
